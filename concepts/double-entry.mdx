## The problem with single-entry

Most people track their finances the way they keep a diary: note when money comes in, note when it goes out, and trust the running total at the bottom. This is **single-entry** accounting.

It feels simple, but it has a fatal flaw: **it has no way of knowing if the total is correct**.

If you write down "spent ₹500 on groceries" you have recorded the expense, but you have not recorded which account that ₹500 left from. Over time, this creates a gap between what your spreadsheet says and what your bank actually shows. You cannot reconcile the two, because the data was never complete to begin with.

The deeper problem is that money always moves *between* places. A single-entry record captures only the destination — it loses the origin. This means your balances cannot be verified, only trusted.

## The double-entry answer

Double-entry bookkeeping solves this by recording **both sides** of every financial movement:

> Every transaction has a source account (where money leaves) and a destination account (where money arrives). The amount leaving one always equals the amount arriving at the other.

This creates a constraint that makes corruption impossible: the books can only balance if every transaction is complete. If something is missing, the imbalance is detectable.

```
You buy groceries for ₹500 using your debit card

  From: Current Account    -₹500
    To: Groceries          +₹500
                           ─────
                               0  ✓ Books balance
```

```
Your salary of ₹75,000 arrives in your bank

  From: Salary            -₹75,000
    To: Current Account   +₹75,000
                          ────────
                                 0  ✓ Books balance
```

## Why this matters for you

**Balances you can trust.** Because every transaction records both sides, Recontrol can reconstruct the exact balance of any account at any point in time — not an estimate, but a mathematically verifiable figure.

**Instant reconciliation.** If your Current Account shows ₹12,400 in Recontrol and ₹12,400 in your bank statement, you know the records agree completely. There is no gap to explain.

**A complete financial picture.** Because money always has a source and a destination, you can see not just what you spent, but what you spent *from* — and how that affected each account individually.

## Debits and credits, plain language

The words "debit" and "credit" carry centuries of accounting convention that can be confusing. In Recontrol, they map to a simpler mental model:

| Journal term | What it means in Recontrol |
|---|---|
| **Debit** | The `from` account — money leaves here |
| **Credit** | The `to` account — money arrives here |

Every transaction in Recontrol has exactly one `from` account (debited) and one `to` account (credited). The debit amount always equals the credit amount. The books always balance.

## A worked example

Suppose you track three accounts:

- **Current Account** (asset): ₹20,000
- **Groceries** (expense): ₹0
- **Salary** (income): ₹0

**1 March — Salary received**

| Account | Change | Balance |
|---|---|---|
| Salary (from) | −₹75,000 | −₹75,000 |
| Current Account (to) | +₹75,000 | +₹95,000 |

**5 March — Buy groceries**

| Account | Change | Balance |
|---|---|---|
| Current Account (from) | −₹2,300 | +₹92,700 |
| Groceries (to) | +₹2,300 | +₹2,300 |

**12 March — Buy groceries again**

| Account | Change | Balance |
|---|---|---|
| Current Account (from) | −₹1,800 | +₹90,900 |
| Groceries (to) | +₹1,800 | +₹4,100 |

**Result after three transactions:**
- Current Account: ₹90,900 — exactly what your bank should show
- Total grocery spend this month: ₹4,100 — exact, no estimation needed
- Salary earned: ₹75,000 — every rupee accounted for

The sum across all accounts is always zero, because every debit has an equal credit. That's the guarantee double-entry provides.
